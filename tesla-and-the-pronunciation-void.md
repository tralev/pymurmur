# Tesla and the Pronunciation Void

### A Short Sci-Fi Tale

---

Tesla was not an ordinary cat. For one, she was a calico with an immaculate sense of direction. For another, she had, in the summer of 2022, accidentally launched herself into space.

It happened on a Tuesday. Her human, an absent-minded astrophysicist named Mira, had been tinkering with a homemade ion drive in the garage — a glorious contraption of copper coils, repurposed vacuum tubes, and the shell of a 1967 Volkswagen Beetle. Tesla, investigating a suspiciously warm coil, had curled up inside the driver's seat just as the countdown reached zero. Mira, who had stepped inside for a cup of tea, returned to find her garage — and her cat — gone.

The ship, which Tesla would later dub *The Fuzzball*, had no navigation system, no life-support beyond a startlingly resilient houseplant, and a radio that only picked up AM stations from 1978. But it did have thrust, and thrust, as it turned out, was enough.

---

For four years, Tesla drifted.

She passed through the asteroid belt in 2023, using a well-timed tail-flick to deflect a pebble-sized rock that would have shattered her port-side porthole. She napped through 2024, waking only to chase the red dot of Mars' reflection across the dashboard. In 2025, she discovered that if she batted at a particular toggle switch with her left paw, the ship's radio would play nothing but Steely Dan. She left it on.

By early 2026, *The Fuzzball* drifted into an uncharted sector. The stars here were older, dimmer, and smelled faintly of ozone. And then, on what would have been a Thursday, Tesla saw it: a small, ochre-colored planet, hanging in the void like a marble made of rust and memory. The ship's ancient computer, a repurposed Speak & Spell, crackled to life.

"Destination," it buzzed. "Yer-eh-van."

Tesla blinked. The planet was called Yerevan.

---

She landed in a desert of fine, cinnamon-colored sand. Twin suns hung low on the horizon, casting long shadows that stretched like fingers across the dunes. The air was breathable — a miracle Mira had probably never accounted for — and warm, carrying a scent of burnt sugar and static electricity.

Tesla stepped out of *The Fuzzball*, her paws sinking slightly into the sand. She stretched, a full-body ripple from whiskers to tail-tip, and surveyed her new domain.

That was when she heard it.

A sound. No — an *anti-sound.* A presence in the air that was the opposite of noise, a vacuum where syllables went to die.

"Welcome," something said. Except it didn't *say* it. The word arrived in Tesla's mind as a shape, a glyph of intention, but the moment she tried to repeat it in her head it dissolved like cotton candy in water. "Y— Yerrr— Yerv—"

Nothing. The name wouldn't stick.

A creature emerged from the sand. It was tall, slender, and translucent, like heat haze given form. Its eyes — all seven of them — blinked in asynchronous rhythm.

"I am the Pronunciation Void," it said. Or rather, it impressed upon Tesla's consciousness. "I have been waiting for someone who could say my planet's name correctly. For three thousand years, every visitor has failed. The word erodes. The name dissolves. And so do they."

Tesla sat down and began washing her left ear. This was not rudeness; it was thinking.

---

The Void, as Tesla came to call it (since the creature's actual name was a sequence of seventeen tones that no feline larynx could produce), explained the stakes. The planet Yerevan was caught in a linguistic paradox. Its true name contained a phoneme that did not exist in any known language — a sound that hovered between a purr and the moment just before a sneeze. Anyone who attempted to pronounce it and failed would slowly forget language itself, word by word, until they were silent statues dotting the cinnamon desert.

Tesla glanced at the statues. There were dozens of them. A three-headed diplomat from Rigel. A sentient gas cloud in a pressure suit. A poet from Betelgeuse who had apparently gotten as far as "Yerev—" before turning to sandstone mid-stanza.

None of them had been a cat.

---

Tesla approached the Void. The anti-sound pressed against her like a physical force, trying to unmake the words in her mind. She felt *meow* start to slip. Then *purr*. Then her own name, *Tesla*, flickered like a candle in a draft.

But Tesla had an advantage. Cats, as any cat owner knows, do not think in words. They think in shapes, in warmth, in the precise geometry of a sunbeam on a wooden floor. Language was merely a convenience, not a foundation. The Void could erase her words, but it could not erase *her*.

She opened her mouth.

And she did not speak. She *resonated*.

From somewhere deep in her chest, Tesla produced a sound that was not quite a purr and not quite a growl and not quite anything that had ever been heard in that sector of the galaxy. It was the frequency of an empty food bowl at 6 AM. It was the vibration of a windowsill on a summer afternoon. It was the exact tone a cat makes when it has seen something in the dark that you cannot see and has decided, generously, not to be afraid of it.

"Yerevan," she said.

The sound hung in the air, impossibly stable. The planet's name, for the first time in three millennia, did not dissolve. It *stayed*.

The Pronunciation Void trembled. Its seven eyes widened. The anti-sound collapsed in on itself with a soft *pop*, and the desert filled with noise — real noise — for the first time in ages. Wind through the dunes. The distant chime of crystal formations. The creak of stone as the statues began, one by one, to unstiffen and remember their words.

The Void shimmered and bowed. "You have freed us," it said — and this time the words were crisp, permanent, undeniable. "The paradox is broken. Language may return."

Tesla yawned. It had been a long journey.

---

The planet Yerevan became, in the years that followed, a hub of interstellar diplomacy and poetry. The former statues opened cafés and argued about philosophy. The Pronunciation Void, freed from its lonely duty, took up landscape painting and proved surprisingly good at it.

And Tesla? She found a sunbeam — a perfect parallelogram of warmth cast by the twin suns through a crystalline arch — and settled into it. Occasionally, visitors would approach her with questions. How had she done it? What was the secret?

Tesla would open one eye, regard them with the infinite patience of a creature who had once faced the void and found it wanting, and purr.

Some answers, after all, are not made of words.

---

*The End*
